Source: CMS NHEA 2024 Fact Sheet. ⓘ Click any row to expand context.
Payer2024 SpendYoY TrendNHE Share
Private Insurance ▼ $1,644.6B▲ +8.8%
31%
🏦
Highest trend rate of any payer in 2024. Hospital contract renewals (+4–6% unit cost) and specialty pharmacy growth are the primary levers. Large employers are absorbing vs. passing to employees.
💊
GLP-1 coverage is now a top-3 cost driver for commercial plans. Formulary control (prior auth, step therapy) is the primary mitigation tool — without it, add $3–8 PMPM.
📈
Per-covered-life cost: ~$7,200. 2024 marks the 3rd consecutive year of accelerating commercial trend — actuaries are projecting 8–9% for 2025 absent structural intervention.
⚠️
Watch: hospital system M&A. Each merger in a concentrated market historically adds 2–5% to commercial rates in that region within 3 years of deal close.
Medicare ▼ $1,118.0B▲ +7.8%
21%
👵
Structural enrollment growth is non-negotiable. ~1.1M new beneficiaries per year through ~2030. Per-beneficiary cost also rising as the average age within the program increases.
🏢
Medicare Advantage now covers 54% of beneficiaries. CMS is tightening MA risk-score oversight — plans that relied on risk-score optimization are facing capitation headwinds in 2025–2026.
💊
IRA Part D $2,000 OOP cap (2025) shifts catastrophic cost to the government and manufacturers. Net effect on program spend is modestly positive for beneficiaries, modestly negative for CMS budgets.
📊
Per-beneficiary cost: ~$14,600. 2× the private insurance per-member figure, reflecting the elderly population's higher chronic condition burden and post-acute care utilization.
Medicaid & CHIP ▼ $931.7B▲ +6.6%
18%
🔄
Enrollment unwinding removed ~6M members post-PHE — but per-enrollee cost rose because the disenrolled population skewed younger and healthier, leaving a sicker average member.
🏠
LTSS is 30% of Medicaid spend and growing faster than acute care. State transitions from nursing home to home-based care (HCBS) are reducing per-episode cost but increasing total utilization.
🧠
Behavioral health is the highest-growth subcategory. 1115 waiver expansions are opening new SUD and mental health benefits — net positive for population health, near-term cost pressure.
📊
Per-enrollee cost varies dramatically: expansion adults ~$3,500/yr vs. aged/disabled ~$22,000/yr. The overall $8,900 average is heavily influenced by the high-cost LTSS population.
Out-of-Pocket ▼ $556.6B▲ +5.9%
11%
📋
55% of covered workers are in HDHPs (2024). First-dollar exposure deters preventive care and medication adherence — particularly among hourly workers and low-income households.
🦷
~$200B of OOP is dental and vision — almost entirely uncovered for the non-Medicare population. Dental benefit expansion is a top employee request and a growing union bargaining point.
💊
IRA OOP cap ($2,000 for Medicare Part D in 2025) provides relief for the Medicare population. Commercial OOP caps remain at plan-level deductible + out-of-pocket max, averaging ~$8,700 for single coverage.
⚠️
OOP growth is slowing (+5.9% vs. +11.4% in 2023) as consumer inflation cools — but HDHP structural growth means OOP as a cost-sharing mechanism continues to expand as a % of household health spending.
Other Payers ▼ $590.5B▼ −7.0%
11%
🏛️
The −7.0% decline is almost entirely PHE wind-down. ~$85B in COVID-era federal supplemental grants, FEMA reimbursements, and emergency appropriations that were in this bucket in 2022–2023 are now gone.
⚕️
VA community care is growing ~12% YoY as the Mission Act expands veteran access to non-VA providers. This partially offsets the PHE decline and is expected to sustain 8–10% growth through 2027.
🪖
TRICARE covers ~9.5M lives at controlled rates. Military health spend grows modestly (+2–3%) relative to civilian trends due to fixed fee schedules and base-access primary care.
📊
Normalized baseline going forward: +2–4% annually. Absent another public health emergency, this category stabilizes as VA and TRICARE growth offsets workers comp normalization.
Source: CMS NHEA 2024 Fact Sheet. All figures CMS actuals.

Important methodology note: Condition-level spending figures span reference years 2019–2024 due to varying data publication cycles across government sources. Dollar figures represent direct medical costs unless otherwise noted; indirect costs (lost productivity) are excluded from chart values to ensure cross-condition comparability. Per-patient costs are derived by dividing total direct costs by estimated prevalence. Payer mix percentages are modeled estimates based on CMS program enrollment by condition, AHRQ MEPS insurance type tabulations, and CMS Medicare/Medicaid chronic condition data. All figures should be treated as approximations for portfolio analytical purposes, not actuarial pricing inputs.

#2 PRIORITY  ·  HIGH POLICY RELEVANCE
Medicare Advantage Financial Performance & Overpayment
Medicare Advantage receives $84B more annually than Traditional Medicare would cost for the same enrollees — $44B (11%) from favorable selection alone. With MA now covering 54% of beneficiaries, this structural overpayment is the dominant federal Medicare cost policy debate. OIG audits found 83–87% of sampled enrollee-years had unsupported diagnosis codes.

#6 PRIORITY  ·  EMPLOYER / PLAN SPONSOR RELEVANCE
Hospital Pricing Transparency
Private insurance pays hospitals an average of 254% of what Medicare pays for the same services. The CMS price transparency rule (2021) has generated machine-readable files from 6,000+ hospitals, but 37% remain non-compliant and GAO found CMS cannot assure data accuracy. For plan sponsors and payers, hospital price benchmarking vs. Medicare rates is a core network strategy input.
